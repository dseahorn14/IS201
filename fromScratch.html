Key Probability Concepts

  1. Conditional Probability
    • Finding the probability of something happening when we already know new information.
    • Example: Updating chances after one door is revealed in the Monty Hall problem.
  2. Simulation Methods
    • Using repeated random trials (like running a game many times) to estimate probabilities.
    • Example: Running the Monty Hall game 1,000 times to see which strategy wins more.
  3. Expected Value
    • The average outcome you would expect if you repeated a random experiment many times.
    • Example: On average, switching doors wins about 2 out of 3 times.
  4. Random Processes
    • Events where outcomes happen unpredictably but still follow probability rules.
    • Example: Coin flips, dice rolls, or randomly choosing doors.

What is the Monty Hall Problem?

The Monty Hall Problem is a probability puzzle based on a game show scenario. There are three doors: behind one is a car (the prize), and behind the other two are goats.

You first pick one door. Then the host, who knows what is behind each door, opens one of the remaining doors to reveal a goat. You are then given a choice: stay with your original door or switch to the other unopened door.

The surprising result is that switching doors gives you a higher chance of winning (about 2/3) compared to staying (about 1/3). This goes against most people's intuition and is a classic example of conditional probability.
